Control


Date: 29 April Updated: 3 July 2016

We have choice!

We can recognize that we have a choice in how we control feelings. Find and create your own path for yourself. Learn and know your own strengths and limits, using your mind thoughts to perform optimally. Try not to react right away. Clarify the situation and find the real focus, which should change the response. Making a habit of the technique to stop, breathe, notice, reflect, and then respond. It should help with negative emotions, instead of being 'triggered' into self-sabotaging reactions.

Competencies to utilise:
> Self-control:
We all could benefit from letting go negative emotions, and not'feeding the monsters'
> Trustworthiness: Acceptance, honesty and integrity. Being sensitive to your own and others changes in the paralymbic brain cortex. Having a non-judgemental attitude.
>Conscientiousness: Having responsibility for personal performance. Managing impulses and internal states to perform optimally.
> Adaptability: Flexibility to handle change, attention deployment, re-interpreting, accepting emotions with humour and curiosity.
> Innovation: Using many diferent methods, striving, trying new approaches and testing strategic ideas.

Thinking and acting differently Controlling our emotions instead of letting them control us, identifying our triggers when they occur. The ‘Siberian North Railroad’ mnemonic will be memorable: > Stop: The sacred pause, stop what ypu are doing...
> Breathe: Concentrate on your breath, focussing on this calms your body
> Notice: Scan yourself, what is happening, what do you feel?
> Reflect: Where do your emotions come from? History? Inadequacy? Why did you respond like this?
> Respond: Be kind, think about how everyone wants to be happy. Respond in a positive and kind manner.

Self awareness practice: It was intense thinking of the situation, and then starting the breathing. From the breathing exercise , I did wander a bit, I was eflecting on the things I was struggling to control, to how I was going to write this blog. Often in reality, situations are entirely out of your control, and I was reflecting when I was a teenager and how things were hard for me as I did not have a clear path for what I wanted to do in life... I think I am slowly improving how to work with myself...and deal with triggers.

My current mindfulness: I use exercise and yoga as therapy to tune into my body and be kind to what it can do. It is another path, as I learn to enjoy the journey in the moment. I am not as fit as I used to be, this is different from my normal fitness training. It is becoming less about performance and more about enjoying the experience. I try to get to yoga class twice a week, it's a bit harder to achieve now, but it is good to unravel the posture from hovering over a computer and to concentrate the breath. It's all about trying to let go for a short while. My head needs it.

This quote helps:
“Wilting flowers do not cause suffering; it is the unrealistic desire that flowers not wilt that causes suffering.”
Thich Nhat Hanh
